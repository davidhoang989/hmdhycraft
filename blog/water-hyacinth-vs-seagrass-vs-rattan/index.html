Water hyacinth vs seagrass vs rattan baskets — a buyer's comparison

14 July 2026·7 min read·Last updated 14 July 2026

In a lookbook, water hyacinth, seagrass, and rattan look interchangeable. In a container, they behave nothing alike. If you are placing an order and only two of the three end up in your retail range, this is the piece that saves you a sample round.

This is written for retail buyers, e-commerce category managers, and importers who source natural-fibre home décor from Vietnam. It covers what each fibre actually is, how each weaves, where it holds shape and where it does not, and which retail category each belongs in.

Water hyacinth — the volume material

Water hyacinth (lục bình in Vietnamese) grows wild across the Mekong Delta. It is technically an invasive aquatic weed, harvested from rivers and canals, sun-dried into long tan stalks, and handwoven into a warm, textured weave.

It is the most affordable of the three. It is also the lightest, which matters when your container costs are going up faster than your unit costs. A 40' HQ container of nesting laundry baskets in water hyacinth ships materially cheaper per unit than the same volume in rattan.

Where it holds up:

  • Storage baskets, set-of-2 and set-of-3. The fuller fibre gives the basket enough body to stand up empty.
  • Laundry baskets. Breathes well, so damp cotton does not stagnate. Lightweight when full.
  • Floor baskets and magazine holders. Warm tone works in living-room contexts, softer than rattan.
  • Seasonal and gift baskets. Takes brown wash, black wash, and white finish cleanly.

Where it does not:

  • Flat serving trays. The chunky weave makes an uneven surface.
  • Structured smaller pieces where dimensions must stay tight. Water hyacinth relaxes slightly over time.
  • Load-bearing pieces without a wire or wood frame inside.

Seagrass — the structured fibre

Seagrass (cói) grows in coastal marshland in north-central Vietnam. Buyers frequently confuse it with grey sedge; they are different plants and behave differently. Cói is finer, rope-like, cooler in tone, and holds tension when woven tight.

It is the fibre I recommend when the piece has to look precise, not just handmade.

Where it holds up:

  • Flat trays and serving pieces. Tight weave gives a flat, level surface.
  • Small structured baskets and organiser cubes. Dimensions stay square through shipping.
  • Place mats, coasters, table runners. Weave finishes cleanly at edges.
  • Pieces where colour matters. Takes black dye and dip-dyed accents better than water hyacinth.

Where it does not:

  • Oversized laundry or floor pieces. Fibre is not full enough to stand up unaided at that scale.
  • Deep brown or heavy wash finishes. Natural cool tone shows through and mutes the colour.
  • Container-heavy programs where you need maximum unit density. Seagrass is slightly denser to pack than water hyacinth.

Rattan — the structural fibre

Rattan is a climbing palm vine, harvested from forests across Southeast Asia. Vietnam is a significant producer, though Indonesia leads regional volume. Rattan is different in kind from the other two: it can be woven flat like water hyacinth, or used as poles that get steamed, bent, and framed into furniture.

It is the premium option in both price and finish. It also takes longer to produce, because the poles have to be prepared before weaving even starts.

Where it holds up:

  • Furniture and furniture accents. Poles bent into frames for chairs, side tables, benches.
  • Rigid planters and heavy storage. Wall thickness carries weight the other fibres cannot.
  • Pieces where visible structure is the design. Rattan cane and rattan poles read as "designed" in a way handwoven fibres do not.
  • Premium retail programs. Buyers who want a step above natural-fibre basketry.

Where it does not:

  • Volume storage programs where price is the constraint. Rattan runs 30 to 60 percent above water hyacinth.
  • Fast lead times. Pole preparation adds two to three weeks.
  • Very tight container packing. Rigid pieces do not nest as well.

Which one for your programme?

Under $10 FOB per unit, volume-driven retail. Water hyacinth almost always wins on unit economics.

Trays, structured smaller pieces, table décor. Seagrass gives a cleaner finish.

Premium home furniture, seasonal decor with structure. Rattan carries the design intent.

Not sure yet? Start with a mixed sample container. Order two SKUs of each fibre against the same design brief and see what your merchandising team responds to before committing to a lane.

What the container looks like in practice

A 40' HQ mixed container from HMD typically holds around 1,200 to 1,800 units of water hyacinth storage baskets, or around 800 to 1,200 units of rattan pieces at similar dimensions. Seagrass sits in between. If your unit landed cost target sits below a threshold, that packing efficiency is often what makes or breaks the P&L.

Peak season in Vietnam runs August to November, driven by holiday retail programs in the US and EU. Rattan books out first because the pole supply is tighter. Water hyacinth stays available longer because the raw material is abundant. If your planning window is short, that alone can decide the fibre.

Choose the fibre that fits the product, not the product that fits the fibre.

For any given design brief, one of the three will be materially better than the other two. The mistake is picking a fibre first, then forcing the design onto it. Send the design; the material choice follows.
